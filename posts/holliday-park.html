Holliday Park's ruins garden gets the grading right

The Ruins at Holliday Park are salvaged limestone facade pieces from a demolished New York building, reassembled here in the 1970s. On paper that sounds like a curiosity. On the ground, it works because of what happens underfoot before you ever reach the stone.

The whole garden sits in a shallow bowl. Every path into it slopes down, gently, so your sightline drops before it lifts back toward the ruins. By the time you're at the base, the columns read taller than they are, and the surrounding tree line frames them instead of competing with them. It's a simple grading trick, but it's done with real discipline — no sudden steps, no retaining wall doing the work that the land should be doing.

What I didn't expect was how well the planting supports the sightlines rather than filling them in. Low grasses and groundcover hold the middle distance; taller canopy trees are pushed to the rim of the bowl. Nothing tall gets planted where it would block the view down. That's an easy rule to state and a hard one to actually hold to over fifty years of maintenance turnover — and it's mostly still holding here.

The bowl does the framing. The plants just know to stay out of the way.

My one complaint: the plaza paving at the base has been patched in at least three different materials over the years, and none of them match the original limestone tone. It's a small thing, but it's the one spot where the design's discipline slips.

Worth a visit if you're in Indianapolis and want to see how far good grading alone can carry a design.
