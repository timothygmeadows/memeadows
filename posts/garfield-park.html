Garfield Park's sunken gardens, sixty years on

The sunken gardens here are old — laid out in the 1910s-20s in the Olmsted Brothers tradition — and they still read clearly as a formal design decades after most of the original planting plan has drifted. That's rarer than it sounds. A strong bones-first layout is the reason.

The garden is dropped about six feet below the surrounding grade, with symmetrical stone stairs on the central axis and a reflecting pool anchoring the far end. Because the geometry is so firmly set in the hardscape, the planting beds around it can wander season to season without the whole thing losing coherence. Annual displays change every year; the structure holding them never has to.

What's drifted is the planting palette itself — more modern annual bedding schemes than the historic plan probably called for, heavier on color, lighter on the structural evergreens that would have anchored the corners originally. It's not wrong, exactly, just a different hand than the one that drew the plan.

The layout is doing sixty years of heavy lifting so the planting doesn't have to.

If you only have time for one formal garden in the city, this is the one. Go in late spring when the bedding displays are freshly planted and the water in the reflecting pool is still clear.
